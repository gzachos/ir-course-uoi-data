Lifehacker is a weblog about life hacks and software that launched on January 31, 2005. The site was originally launched by Gawker Media and is currently owned by G/O Media. The blog posts cover a wide range of topics including: Microsoft Windows, Mac, Linux programs, iOS and Android, as well as general life tips and tricks. The website is known for its fast-paced release schedule from its inception, with content being published every half hour all day long.[2] The Lifehacker motto is "Tips, tricks, and downloads for getting things done."[3]

In addition, Lifehacker has four international editions, Lifehacker Australia, Lifehacker Japan, Lifehacker India and Lifehacker UK which feature most posts from the U.S. edition along with extra content specific to local readers.[4][5]

History[edit]

Gina Trapani founded Lifehacker and was the site's sole blogger until September 2005, when two associate editors joined her, Erica Sadun and D. Keith Robinson.[6] Other former associate editors include Wendy Boswell, Rick Broida,[7] Jason Fitzpatrick, Kevin Purdy, and Jackson West.[8] Former contributing editors include The How-To Geek,[9] and Tamar Weinberg.[10]

Lifehacker launched in January 2005 with an exclusive sponsorship by Sony. The highly publicized ad campaign was rumored to have cost $75,000 for three months.[11] Since then, a variety of tech-oriented advertisers have appeared on the site.[12]

Lifehacker's frequent guest posts have included articles by Joe Anderson, Eszter Hargittai, Matt Haughey, Meg Hourihan, Jeff Jarvis.[13][14][15][16][17]

On January 16, 2009, Trapani resigned as Lifehacker's lead editor and Adam Pash assumed the position.[18]

On February 7, 2011, Lifehacker revealed a redesigned site with a cleaner layout.[19] Then, on April 15, 2013, Lifehacker redesigned their site again to match the other newly redesigned Gawker sites, like Kotaku.[20]

On January 7, 2013, Adam Pash moved on from Lifehacker to a new start-up, and Whitson Gordon became the new editor-in-chief.[21]

On January 1, 2016, Whitson Gordon parted ways with Lifehacker to another popular technology website, How-To Geek, as their editor-in-chief [22] replacing Lowell Heddings.[23] In his announcement, Gordon confirmed that Alan Henry would take over as the interim editor pending interviewing processes. Alan Henry became the new editor-in-chief on February 1, 2016.

On February 3, 2017, Alan Henry left his position at Lifehacker. He has since moved on to write for the New York Times.[24]

On February 28, 2017, Melissa Kirsch became the editor-in-chief.[25]

Lifehacker was one of six websites that was purchased by Univision Communications in their acquisition of Gawker Media in August 2016.[26]

Podcast[edit]

Lifehacker staff ran the Ask Lifehacker podcast, which was discontinued in April 2014. From May 2014, former Lifehacker writer Adam Dachis hosted Supercharged, a podcast with the same theme and set-up, on which Lifehacker writers Alan Henry, Whitson Gordon, Eric Ravenscraft, Thorin Klosowski and Patrick Allen frequently co-hosted.

As of January 2017, Lifehacker has a weekly podcast called The Upgrade. It is hosted by Melissa Kirsch and Alice Bradley and features experts and Lifehacker staff "helping you improve your life, one week at a time".[27]

According to this letter from the editor on Lifehacker, Alan Henry will no longer be the Editor-in-Chief at Lifehacker and has since joined The New York Times. Gizmodo Media announced Melissa Kirsch as his replacement in February 2017.

Accolades[edit]

  • In 2005, TIME named Lifehacker one of the "50 Coolest Web Sites"[30] in 2005, one of the "25 Sites We Can't Live Without"[31] in 2006 and one of the "25 Best Blogs 2009"[32]
  • CNET named Lifehacker in their "Blog 100" in October 2005.[33]
  • Wired presented Gina Trapani with a Rave Award in 2006 for Best Blog.[34]
  • In the 2007 Weblog Awards, Lifehacker was awarded Best Group Weblog.[35]
  • PC Magazine named Lifehacker in "Our Favorite 100 Blogs" in October 2007.[36]
  • US Mensa named Lifehacker as one of their top 50 sites in 2010.[37]
